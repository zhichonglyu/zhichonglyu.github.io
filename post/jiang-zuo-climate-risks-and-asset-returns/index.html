【讲座】Climate Risks and Asset Returns

2023-03-21 14 min read

1 Background

2 Investors and Regulator's View on Climate Risks

2.1 Regulator's View on Climate Risks

  Governments and Regulators worldwide are concerned about the potential systematic risks imposed by climate change on financial stability
  Network for Greening the Financial System,Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures, Climate Risk Disclosure Act of 2019, etc.
  "It is  an existential threat to our environment, and it poses a tremendous risk to our country's financial stability.We know that storms will hit us with more frequency, and more intensity. We know warming temperatures might disrupt food and water supplies, leading to unrest around the world. Our financial system must be prepared for the market and credit risks of these climate-related events. But it must also be prepared for the best-possible case scenario: that we begin a rapid transition to a net-zero carbon economy,which also creates potential challenges for financial institutions and market.  — Secretary of the Treasury of the United States, 2021

2.2 Institutional Investor's Views on Climate Risks

  Kruger,Sautner and Starks(RFS 2020): a global survey of 439 institutional investors and their views on climate risks
    institutional investors believe climate risks have financial implications for their portfolio firms and that the risks have already begun to materialize
    consider risk management and engagement,rather than divestment to be the better approach for addressing climate risks
    believe that some equity valuations do not fully reflect climate risks 
    believe climate risk reporting to be as important as traditional financial reporting and that it should be mandatory and more standardized.
  Stroebel and Wurgler (JFE 2021) survey 861 finance academics,professionals, and public sector regulators and policy economists about climate finance topics 
  Most respondents believe that asset prices underestimate climate risks rather than overestimate them

3 Measuring and Pricing Climate Risks

3.1 The Impact of Climate Risks on Asset Prices: Channels

  Climate change affects asset prices mainly in two ways: 
  Physical risks: direct costs result from the adverse effects of climate change on economic activity (extreme weather events, sea-level rises etc.)
  E.g.,fossil-fuel companies can be adversely affected by carbon taxes or limits on carbon emissions technological innovations accelerated by climate policies could threaten the business models of firms that operate in traditional industries
  Transition risks:costs imposed on firms from policies and regulations implemented to combat climate change and transit towards a low-carbon economy 
  E.g., insurance companies'exposures to higher losses from insured properties in coastal areas and food producers'exposures to sustained drought spells
  Rational theories: assets with higher exposure to climate risks should earn higher average returns
  Behavioral finance models: assets with higher exposure to climate risks could earn lower (higher ) future returns if investors underreact (overreact) to the economic impacts of climate risks

3.2 Is Climate Risks Priced?

  Recent studies start to explore whether financial markets sufficiently price in the long-run risk of climate change 
  Why is this important?
    Pricing climate risks properly today reduces the possibility of wealth transfers between uninformed and sophisticated investors
    and the likelihood of extreme price movements in future
  The short answer: it depends!
    Type of climate risks (physical vs.transition; short-term vs. long-term)
    Asset class
    Investor attention
    Investor beliefs

3.3 Measuring Physical Climate Risks

  Physical climate risks can be measured by the severity or losses caused by natural disasters, extreme temperatures, projected loss due to sea-level rise etc.
   Sometimes it is not just the realization of climate risk,but also our perception/concerns about climate change,that affect asset prices
  Engle et al.(RFS 2020) construct a climate change news index that captures the intensity of discussions about climate change in WSJ

3.4 Measuring Individual Firm's Exposure to Climate Risks

  Generally two approaches to estimate individual assets'or firms exposure to climate risks, similar to how we measure macroeconomic risk exposure
  Regression-based approach:
    Regressing returns of individual stocks or bonds on measures of climate risk to get "climate risk beta"
    Easy to implement,less requirement on data,and can be estimated for a large cross-section of assets
    But beta estimates are usually noisy, not stable and backward looking
  Characteristics-based approach:
    Use the industry affiliation or location of assets combined with realized or forecasted climate risks
    For example,firms in the food sector or located closer to disaster zones have higher climate risk exposure
    Similarly, real estates closer to seaside more exposed to sea-level rise risks
    Sautner et al.(2022)apply textual analysis to earnings conference call to construct time-varying measures of how market participants perceive firms exposures to climate change

3.5 Is Physical Climate Risk Priced

  Empirical studies have documented both underreaction and overreaction to (physical)climate risks in financial markets 
  Stock markets
    Agricultural firms' stock prices underreact to prolonged drought risks (Hong,Li,and Xu,2019) Hong,Li,and Xu (JE 2019):Global stock markets under-react to climate induced droughts
      A long-term trend towards drought for a country forecasts relatively poor profit growth and stock returns for companies in the food industry
      A cross-country long-short strategy earns a 4-factor alpha of 0.58% per month
    Mutual fund managers overreact to large climatic disasters by underweighting disaster zone stocks (Alok,Kumar,and Wermers, RFS2020)
   Bond markets
    Municipal bonds issued by counties exposed to sea-level rise have higher yields (Painter,JFE 2020) Painter (JFE 2020):The municipal bond maket provides a useful setting to study this question
      Immobile
      Heterogeneity in term structure and exposure to climate change across counties
    Hallegatte et al.(2013)sea-level rise risk measure: expected mean annual loss from sea level rise as a percentage of GDP
    Corporate bonds that can better hedge against climate change news risk earn lower average returns (Huynh and Xia,JFQA 2021)
    Projected climate change damage affects yields for sovereign bonds with long maturities (Barnett and Yannelis,2021)
  Real Estates markets
    Real estates in coastal areas sell at a discount due to sea-level rise (Bernstein et al.,RFS 2020; Baldauf et al.,RFS 2020)
    Does the residential real estate market price climate risk?
      Murfin and Spiegel (RFS 2020)
        No
        Measure sea level rise(SLR)risk using a continuous measure based on home's elevation and rates of subsidence/land rebound (Source-NOAA)
      Bernstein,Gustafson,and Lewis(JFE 2019)
        Yes, for sophisticated investors
        Calculate SLR risk using dummy variables based on one foot increments (NOAA)
      Baldauf,Garlappi,and Yannelis(RFS 2020)
        Yes, if you believe in it 
        Dummy variable indicating if NOAA has designated a home at risk if seas rise six feet or more
      The impact SLR on residential real estate not ony affect home owner but also mortgage lenders
        Duan and Li (2020): mortgage lenders cut lending amounts and approve fewer loans in a region when their concerns for climate change rise

3.6 What is Climate Transition Risks

  As climate change is mostly caused by accumulations of greenhouse gases (GHG) in earth's atmosphere, any regulation will have to target at significantly curbing firms'carbon emissions
  The most effective way to reduce GHG emission is to put a price on carbon emissions and internalize the exteralities associated with emissions

3.7 EU Emission s Trading System

  Emissions Trading System (ETS) is the EU's main tool for reducing greenhouse gas emissions that cause climate change
  The ETs is designed to put a cost on carbon dioxide for the region's most highly polluting industries,from aviation to mining
  It currently covers around 40% of the EU's greenhouse gas emissions

3.8 Emission Trading Schemes in China

  In July 2021,China's national emissions trading system(ETS) was launched
  The world's largest single-country carbon market,covering 4,000 MtCO2 or 30-40%of its national GHG emissions 
  Built on the successful implementation of 8 pilot ETS at the regional level
    Beijing,Shanghai,Tianjin,Chongqing,Shenzhen,Guangdong,Hubei,and Fujian
    Follow the principle of cap and trade
  Covering 2,200+companies from Power sector (including combined heat and power,as well as captive power plants of other sectors)
  Expected to be gradually expanded to cover seven other sectors: petrochemical,chemical,building materials,steel,nonferrous metals, paper, and domestic aviation

3.11 Pricing of Climate Transition Risks in Stock and Option Markets

  Bolton and Kacperczyk (JFE 2021,JF 2022): higher stock returns associated with higher levels and growth rates of carbon emissions in both US and around the world
    Carbon premia are greater for firms located in countries with lower economic development,larger energy sectors,and less inclusive political systems
  Ilhan,Sautner,and Vilkov (RFS 2020): Climate policy uncertainty is priced in the option market
    Cost of option protection against downside tail risks is larger for firms with more carbon-intense business models
    Effect is magnified at times when the public's attention to climate change spikes
  These studies suggest that investors are already demanding compensation for their exposure to carbon emission risk

3.12 Do Corporate Bonds Price in Climate Transition Risks?

  Duan,Li,and Wen (JFQA forthcoming) examine whether there is a "carbon risk premium" in US corporate bond market
    The paper finds strong evidence contrary to the "Carbon Risk Premium" hypothesis in US corporate bond 
      High-CEl bonds are riskier than low-CEl bonds, as reflected in higher bond narket beta and downside risk,lower credit rating and liquidity
      Yet high-CEl bonds underperform by 1.7% (t=2.62) per year relative to low-CEl bonds
      The "low carbon premium" cannot be explained by systematic risks and other bond characteristics
    At June of each year t,we form quintile portfolios of corporate bonds within each of the FF12 industries,based on firm-level carbon intensity in fiscal year t-1
    Calculate value-weighted portfolio returns from July of year t to June of year t+1, and average across industries
    What theories could explain the "low carbon alpha" in the data?
      “Investor Preference'” hypothesis:
        Green assets could perform better than brown assets if investors'ESG concerns strengthen unexpectedly (Pastor,Stambaugh,and Taylor, JFE 2021)
        We test this explanation and find institutional investors indeed divest from bonds issued by carbon-intensive firms
        However,the "low carbon alpha" cannot be fully explained by shifts in institutional demand
      “Investor Underreaction” hypothesis:
        Green assets could earn higher returns if being carbon efficient indicates strong firm fundamentals,and the market underreacts to this predictability of fundamentals (Pedersen,Fitzgibbons,and Pomorski, JFE 2021)
        We find supporting evidence as high CEl predicts lower future cash flow news,deteriorating firm creditworthiness,and more negative environmental ncidents
    "Investor Underreaction" hypothesis: investors underract to the predictability of CEl for firm fundamentals
    We use three proxies for firm cash flow news:
      standardized unexpected earnings SUE
      standardized unexpected revenue growth estimator SURGE (Jegadeesh and Livnat,2006)
      cumulative abnormal return in a four-day window around earnings announcements (CAR(-2, +1))
    Carbon Intensity and Firm Creditworthiness 
      The previous results suggest firms with lower (higher) CEl are associated with better (poorer) future fundamentals
      Better(poorer)fundamentals could lead to improving (deteriorating) firm creditworthiness,which then drive the higher (lower) realized returns of low (high) CEl bonds
      Two proxies for firm creditworthiness:
        change of bond-level credit ratings (ARating)
        change of Ohlson's O-Score (△O_Scorei,t+1). A higher O-score indicates a higher probabMty of financial distress
      Carbon-intensive firms experience deteriorated creditworthiness subsequently
  Why bond markets?
    Corporate bonds are mainly held by institutional investors,who are sophisticated and likely take carbon risks into account (Krueger,Sautner, and Starks,2020)
    Climate policies mainly constitute as a downside risk to carbon intensive firms (Ilhan,Sautner,and Vilkov,2020),thus should matter more for bond value
    Debt financing forms a significant portion of firms'capital structures, underscoring the need to study how carbon emissions affect a firm's cost of debt financing

4 Conclusion

Institutional investors have growing concerns toward climate risk 
The question of whether financial markets recognize climate risks depends on the salience of the risk and investor beliefs
  Research also consistently finds markets are getting better at pricing climate change over time,especially after Paris Agreement in 2015
It is early days in finding solutions to climate change,but policies need to internalize potential externalities and factor in the market's potential to recognize climate risk
